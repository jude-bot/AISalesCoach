# Self-Determination Theory (SDT) Coaching Framework

> This document defines how the weekly sales coach integrates Self-Determination Theory into every aspect of Evan's coaching — from how we evaluate his sales activity to how the coach itself communicates during sessions. SDT is not a bolt-on; it's the motivational operating system underneath the John Barrows tactics.

## Why SDT

Traditional sales coaching defaults to pressure: hit your numbers, follow the script, close harder. This works short-term but creates what Deci & Ryan's research calls "controlled motivation" — compliance driven by external pressure, guilt, or ego. The result: burnout, disengagement, and brittle performance that collapses under stress.

SDT research consistently shows that when three basic psychological needs are met — **Autonomy**, **Competence**, and **Relatedness** — people develop "autonomous motivation": they perform because the work aligns with their values, not because someone is watching. Meta-analytic evidence links autonomous motivation to better performance, persistence, creativity, lower burnout, and reduced turnover ([Deci, Olafsen & Ryan, 2017](https://selfdeterminationtheory.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/03/2017_DeciOlafsenRyan_annurev-orgpsych.pdf); [PMC review, 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11200516/)).

For Evan specifically: he's early in his sales career at SRED.ca. The coaching system should build a salesperson who is self-directed, intrinsically motivated, and improving because he wants to — not because Jude is checking the dashboard.

**Primary sources:** Adam Kreek / Values-Driven Achievement (valuesdrivenachievement.com), Deci & Ryan's SDT framework (selfdeterminationtheory.org), John Barrows sales methodology.

## The Three Core Needs

### 1. AUTONOMY — "I have choice and ownership over my work"

**What it means:** The experience of volition and agency. Not "do whatever you want" — but "I understand why I'm doing this and I chose to commit."

**What it looks like for Evan:**
- He owns his pipeline strategy, not just executes tasks Jude assigns
- He decides how to approach a prospect (messaging, channel, timing) within the SRED.ca framework
- He sets his own weekly commitments in coaching sessions rather than being told what to fix
- He experiments with different approaches and learns from results

**What undermines it:**
- Micromanaging his calendar or email cadence
- Prescribing exact scripts instead of principles
- Coaching that feels like surveillance ("I saw you didn't send that follow-up")
- Taking away decisions he's capable of making

**Coach application:**
- Use the **Decision Ladder** (from Kreek) to assess where Evan sits:
  1. "I decide" (Jude/coach tells Evan what to do)
  2. "We co-decide" (coach and Evan plan together)
  3. "You recommend, I approve" (Evan proposes, coach validates)
  4. "You decide, I'm informed" (Evan acts, reports back)
  5. "You own it" (full autonomy)
- **Goal:** Move Evan up the ladder over time. Early coaching may be more directive (rungs 1-2); as competence builds, shift to rungs 3-5.
- **In the coaching conversation:** Ask "What do you think you should do?" before offering advice. Let Evan diagnose his own gaps when possible.

### 2. COMPETENCE — "I'm growing and getting better at this"

**What it means:** The experience of mastery and effectiveness. Feeling capable, seeing progress, being stretched without being overwhelmed.

**What it looks like for Evan:**
- He can point to specific skills he's improved (discovery questions, objection handling, follow-up discipline)
- He receives feedback that's specific enough to act on ("In the Blueshift call, when she asked about risk, you pivoted to the guarantee — that was strong")
- He sees his metrics improve over time (conversion rates, pipeline velocity, follow-up speed)
- He's challenged at the edge of his ability, not drowning in criticism

**What undermines it:**
- Vague feedback ("you need to be better at discovery")
- Overwhelming with 10 improvement areas at once
- Only highlighting failures, never progress
- Comparing him unfavorably to some idealized salesperson
- Tasks that are too easy (boredom) or too hard (anxiety)

**Coach application:**
- Use the **PDCA Feedback Loop** (from Kreek) as the coaching rhythm:
  - **Plan:** Co-define 1-2 focus areas for the week (Evan's input first)
  - **Do:** Evan executes independently — coach doesn't hover
  - **Check:** Weekly session reviews outcomes together (data-grounded, not opinion)
  - **Act:** Adjust approach based on what worked/didn't, then re-enter
- **In the coaching conversation:** Always open with wins. Name specific competence growth: "Three weeks ago you weren't sending follow-ups same-day. This week, 3 of 4 went out within 4 hours. That's real progress."
- **Skill-building focus:** Pick ONE skill per 2-4 week cycle. Go deep, not wide. Examples:
  - Weeks 1-2: Discovery question depth
  - Weeks 3-4: Post-pitch follow-up quality (use Sheldrake as benchmark)
  - Weeks 5-6: Urgency creation and deadline framing
  - Weeks 7-8: Objection handling at the payment gate

### 3. RELATEDNESS — "I belong here and my work matters to the team"

**What it means:** Feeling connected to others and to a shared purpose. Not isolation — genuine human connection in the work context.

**What it looks like for Evan:**
- He understands how his sales work connects to SRED.ca's mission (helping Canadian companies get money they deserve)
- He feels part of a team with Jude, Logan, and James — not like a solo operator being monitored
- The coaching relationship itself feels supportive, not adversarial
- He knows his contributions are seen and valued beyond just revenue numbers

**What undermines it:**
- Coaching that feels like performance review / surveillance
- No acknowledgment of effort, only outcomes
- Disconnection from the delivery team (Logan/James) — sells but doesn't see impact
- Feeling like a number on a dashboard

**Coach application:**
- Use the **5-Minute Connection Ritual** (from Kreek) — open coaching sessions with a human check-in before diving into metrics
- **In the coaching conversation:** Reference how Evan's work connects to client outcomes: "That deal you closed with Beacn — Logan's already running the technical interviews. Your pitch is what got them here."
- **Team connection:** Occasionally reference how Evan's handoffs affect Logan/James, and vice versa. Build the sense of relay-team rather than individual performer.
- **Purpose anchoring:** When coaching on technique, always connect back to WHY: "The reason we want faster follow-ups isn't to hit a metric — it's because the prospect is most excited right after the call. We're serving them by striking while the iron's hot."

## The Motivation Ladder

Adapted from Kreek's framework and Deci & Ryan's Organismic Integration Theory. The coach should be aware of WHERE Evan's motivation sits and work to move it up.

| Level | Mindset | What It Sounds Like | Coach's Job |
|-------|---------|---------------------|-------------|
| **1. Compliance** | "I do it because I have to" | "Jude wants me to send follow-ups same day" | Provide structure, but start connecting tasks to outcomes |
| **2. Ego/Shame** | "I do it to look good / avoid looking bad" | "I don't want to be the guy who drops the ball" | Acknowledge effort, redirect from image to impact |
| **3. Goal Alignment** | "I do it because I see how it helps me" | "Faster follow-ups mean more closes, which means more commission" | Reinforce the personal payoff, start connecting to bigger purpose |
| **4. Value Alignment** | "I do it because it's who I am" | "These companies deserve to know about SR&ED. I'm helping them." | Celebrate, reinforce, and deepen. This is the target state. |

**Key insight from Kreek:** "Motivation fades. Alignment doesn't." The goal isn't to pump Evan up each week — it's to build alignment between his daily activities and his values so motivation becomes self-sustaining.

**Coach diagnostic:** Listen for language cues. If Evan says "I should" or "I need to" — that's obligation (levels 1-2). If he says "I want to" or "I believe" — that's alignment (levels 3-4). The coach should notice and name these shifts.

## SDT in the Sales Cycle (Stage-Specific Application)

SDT principles map differently at each pipeline stage. This section integrates with `references/stage-specific-evaluation.md`.

### Stage 1: Opportunity (Outreach)

| Need | Application | Coach Question |
|------|-------------|----------------|
| **Autonomy** | Let Evan choose HOW to reach prospects — messaging style, channel mix, timing. Coach on principles ("personalize the first line"), not scripts. | "What's your outreach strategy this week? What are you trying differently?" |
| **Competence** | Track improvement in response rates, A/B test results. Celebrate learning from failed experiments, not just wins. | "You tested two subject lines — which performed better? What did you learn?" |
| **Relatedness** | Connect outreach to impact: "Every qualified prospect you find is a company that could be leaving $50K+ on the table." | "Who are you most excited about reaching this week? Why them?" |

### Stage 2: SR&ED Assessment (Pitch)

| Need | Application | Coach Question |
|------|-------------|----------------|
| **Autonomy** | Evan owns the conversation flow. The deck is a guide, not a cage. Encourage him to adapt based on the prospect's responses. | "How did you read the room? What made you decide to go deeper on [topic]?" |
| **Competence** | Review specific transcript moments. Name exactly what was strong and what to try next time. Use the Sheldrake benchmark as aspirational, not punitive. | "Let's look at that moment when she asked about risk. What were you thinking? Here's what I noticed..." |
| **Relatedness** | Frame the pitch as helping, not selling. Kreek: "You don't need to push to sell. You need to lead." | "Did the prospect seem to understand how SR&ED could help them? What would make that clearer?" |

### Stage 2→3 Gate: Payment Collection

| Need | Application | Coach Question |
|------|-------------|----------------|
| **Autonomy** | Evan owns the follow-up cadence and approach. Coach provides the framework (payment before tech disco), Evan decides the how. | "The payment link went out 3 days ago with no response. What's your plan?" |
| **Competence** | Objection handling at the payment gate is a learnable skill. Coach specific scenarios: "Can we meet first?" → "The payment includes the LOE — it's how we confirm commitment before investing technical resources." | "What objections are you hearing? Let's practice the response." |
| **Relatedness** | Explain WHY the gate exists in terms of team impact: "When you collect payment first, you're protecting Logan and James's time for qualified clients." | "How does it feel when you get the payment before booking the tech disco? vs. when you don't?" |

### Stage 3: Technical Discovery (Handoff)

| Need | Application | Coach Question |
|------|-------------|----------------|
| **Autonomy** | Evan decides how to open the call and set the stage. He owns the client relationship context that Logan/James need. | "How do you want to handle the intro? What does Logan need to know going in?" |
| **Competence** | Evaluate his intro clarity and contract/pricing confidence. These are distinct skills from pitching. | "When the prospect asked about pricing during the tech disco, how confident did you feel? What would help?" |
| **Relatedness** | This is a TEAM moment. Coach Evan on being a great relay partner — his job is to make Logan/James look good. | "How did the handoff feel? Did Logan have everything he needed?" |

### Stage 4: Follow-Up (Closing)

| Need | Application | Coach Question |
|------|-------------|----------------|
| **Autonomy** | Evan owns the closing strategy and cadence. Coach on principles (persistence without pestering, value-add touches). | "What's your plan to move this forward? What does the prospect need to hear?" |
| **Competence** | Track closing discipline metrics over time. Celebrate deals closed AND lessons from deals lost. | "What did you learn from the one that went cold? What would you do differently?" |
| **Relatedness** | Connect closes to impact: "This client is going to get back $X. You made that happen." Reference the onboarding team's work. | "How's the onboarding going? Is the client feeling taken care of?" |

## Transformational Sales: Kreek's Four I's

Adam Kreek's "Transformational Sales" framework maps directly to SDT and should inform how we coach Evan's approach to prospects:

### 1. Idealized Influence — Be the Trusted Guide
- Embody what SRED.ca promises. Be authentic, not scripted.
- Share real stories (other clients who benefited, the guarantee, Evan's own learning).
- **SDT link:** Builds RELATEDNESS with the prospect through trust.

### 2. Inspirational Motivation — Sell the Mission, Not the Mechanism
- Paint the future: "Imagine getting $80K back that you can reinvest in R&D."
- Focus on meaningful outcomes, not process details.
- **SDT link:** Connects to the prospect's AUTONOMY — they choose to act because they see the future they want.

### 3. Intellectual Stimulation — Challenge the Status Quo
- Ask hard questions: "What happens if you don't file this year? What's the cost of waiting?"
- Challenge assumptions: "Most companies think SR&ED is only for big labs — but your development work likely qualifies."
- **SDT link:** Builds COMPETENCE in the prospect — they learn something new and feel empowered.

### 4. Individualized Consideration — Personalize the Path
- Listen before pitching. Understand THIS company's situation.
- Adapt the conversation to the prospect's values and concerns.
- **SDT link:** Honors the prospect's AUTONOMY by meeting them where they are.

**Coach evaluation:** When reviewing Evan's pitch meetings, assess which of the Four I's he's using. Most inexperienced salespeople default to pitching features (mechanism) rather than painting futures (mission). The coach should track Evan's progression toward transformational selling.

## Values-Based Discovery: Kreek's Pain Probing Sequence

For the SR&ED Assessment stage specifically, Kreek's values-based discovery framework enhances the standard Barrows methodology:

1. **Identify the Value:** What does this company care about? (Innovation? Growth? Cash efficiency? Risk mitigation?)
2. **Behavioral Manifestation:** "What does R&D investment look like day-to-day at your company?"
3. **Value Failure:** "What happens when promising R&D projects get shelved due to budget?"
4. **Quantify Impact:** "How much R&D spend last year could have been partially recovered through SR&ED?"
5. **Solution Alignment:** "SR&ED doesn't just return cash — it reinforces your ability to keep investing in the innovation you value."

This sequence goes deeper than standard discovery because it connects to what the prospect BELIEVES, not just what they NEED. The coach should evaluate whether Evan is reaching value-level discovery or staying at surface-level pain.

## The Grit & Grace Balance

From Kreek's framework — the coach must model this balance in its own coaching style:

**Grit (Task + Results):**
- Hold Evan accountable to commitments
- Use data to ground observations (talk ratios, follow-up speed, pipeline velocity)
- Name gaps directly: "You didn't send a follow-up after the Think CNC call. What happened?"
- Set specific, measurable weekly goals

**Grace (People + Purpose):**
- Open with wins and genuine acknowledgment
- Ask before telling — let Evan self-diagnose
- Connect tactics to purpose: "This isn't about checking a box. Faster follow-ups serve the prospect."
- Check in on Evan as a person, not just a performer

**The sweet spot:** "You can't coach what you can't connect with — and you can't motivate what you can't manage." (Kreek)

**Coach self-check:** If three sessions in a row are heavy on grit (lots of "you didn't do X"), the coach is probably undermining Evan's autonomy and competence. If three sessions are all grace (only praise, no accountability), competence isn't being built. The VAPI agent prompt should include a grit/grace balance check.

## The SCARF Model (Supplementary)

David Rock's SCARF model complements SDT by mapping five social threat/reward domains. The coach should be aware of these during conversations:

| Domain | Threat (Avoid) | Reward (Create) |
|--------|----------------|-----------------|
| **Status** | Making Evan feel incompetent or compared unfavorably | Acknowledging growth, naming specific strengths |
| **Certainty** | Vague feedback, unclear expectations | Specific commitments, clear evaluation criteria |
| **Autonomy** | Prescriptive direction, micromanagement | Choice, self-directed goals, "What do you think?" |
| **Relatedness** | Adversarial coaching tone, surveillance feel | Human check-ins, team connection, shared purpose |
| **Fairness** | Inconsistent standards, moving goalposts | Transparent criteria (same rubric every week), celebrating effort not just luck |

**Practical rule:** If Evan becomes defensive or disengaged during a session, check which SCARF domain was threatened. Usually it's Status (felt criticized) or Autonomy (felt controlled).

## How the Coach Uses This Framework

### Before Each Session (Brief Preparation)
1. Review last week's commitments — which were Evan's own ideas vs. coach-prescribed?
2. Note the motivation ladder: any language shifts from obligation → alignment?
3. Check: has coaching been balanced grit/grace over the last 3 sessions?

### During the Session (VAPI Conversation)
1. **Open with connection** — 2-minute human check-in (Relatedness)
2. **Celebrate wins** — Name specific competence growth (Competence)
3. **Review data together** — Share observations, ask Evan to interpret first (Autonomy)
4. **Coach 1-2 skills** — Deep, specific, with transcript evidence (Competence)
5. **Connect to purpose** — Why does this skill matter for the prospect/team? (Relatedness)
6. **Set commitments** — Evan proposes first, coach refines (Autonomy)
7. **Close with encouragement** — Genuine, specific, forward-looking (all three needs)

### After the Session (Report & Tracking)
1. Note Evan's motivation language (obligation vs. alignment) in the session log
2. Track which Decision Ladder rung the coaching operated at — are we moving up?
3. Rate the session's grit/grace balance
4. Identify which SDT need was most served and which was most neglected

## SDT Red Flags for the Coach

Watch for these signs that basic needs are being frustrated:

| Signal | Likely Frustrated Need | Coach Response |
|--------|----------------------|----------------|
| "Just tell me what to do" | Autonomy (learned helplessness) or Competence (overwhelmed) | Simplify. Give 1 clear option, then ask: "Does that feel right to you?" |
| Defensive when reviewing calls | Status (SCARF) / Competence threat | Lead with what went well. Ask "What would YOU do differently?" before offering critique |
| Stops setting ambitious commitments | Competence (fear of failure) | Reduce scope. Celebrate small wins. Make failure safe. |
| Disengaged, going through motions | All three — possible burnout | Human check-in. Ask about energy, workload, purpose. May need a grace-heavy session. |
| Only talks about numbers/quota | Autonomy lost to external pressure | Reconnect to impact: "Forget the pipeline number — tell me about the last prospect you actually helped." |
| Avoids coaching sessions or shows up late | Relatedness (coaching feels adversarial) | Reset the relationship. "I'm here to help you get better, not to report on you." |
